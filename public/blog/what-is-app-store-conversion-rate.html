What Is App Store Conversion Rate? Definition, Benchmarks & How to Improve It

July 2026 8 min read All posts

App Store conversion rate is the percentage of people who visit your product page and actually tap "Get" to download your app. You calculate it by dividing total downloads by total product page views, then multiplying by 100. The average conversion rate across all App Store categories sits between 25% and 35%, according to Apple's own App Store Connect benchmarks.

For indie developers, this single metric tells you whether your product page is doing its job. You can rank #1 for a keyword and still lose most potential users if your screenshots, description, or ratings don't convince visitors to hit download. Conversion rate is the bridge between visibility and growth.

Key takeaways
  • Conversion rate = (Downloads ÷ Product Page Views) × 100
  • Average across categories: 25–35%. Above 35% is excellent; below 20% signals a problem.
  • Apple uses conversion rate as a ranking signal — higher conversion → higher keyword rankings.
  • Screenshots and the first 3 lines of your description drive 80%+ of the conversion decision.
  • Track conversion rate weekly in App Store Connect → Analytics → Metrics.

How App Store Conversion Rate Is Calculated

The formula is straightforward:

Conversion Rate = (Total Downloads ÷ Total Product Page Views) × 100

Apple counts a "product page view" each time a unique user opens your app's page on the App Store — whether they arrive from search results, a browse category, a referral link, or a featured placement. A "download" is counted when the user taps Get (or the price button for paid apps) and the download begins.

Two things trip people up:

  1. Re-downloads don't count. If a user who already has your app visits the page, that's a view but not a conversion. This means your rate can dip after a press mention or viral moment that drives curious-but-already-installed traffic.
  2. Impressions ≠ product page views. An impression happens when your app appears in search results. The user hasn't tapped through yet. Conversion rate only measures people who made it to your actual page. If you want the full funnel, you need to track impression-to-page-view rate separately.

You can find your conversion rate in App Store Connect → Analytics → Metrics → Conversion Rate. Filter by source type (App Store Search, App Store Browse, App Referrer, Web Referrer) to see which channels convert best.

A useful rule of thumb: if your conversion rate is in the bottom quartile for your category, you're leaving 40–60% of potential downloads on the table. For an indie app getting 500 product page views per week, moving from 20% to 30% conversion means 50 extra downloads every week — 2,600 more per year — without spending a dollar on acquisition.

Why Conversion Rate Matters for App Store Rankings

Apple's search algorithm doesn't just look at keywords and metadata. It watches how users behave. When your app converts a high percentage of product page visitors into downloads, Apple interprets that as a relevance and quality signal. The logic: if 40% of people who see your page download it, your app probably matches what searchers want.

This creates a compounding loop:

  1. Better product page → higher conversion rate
  2. Higher conversion rate → Apple ranks you higher for target keywords
  3. Higher ranking → more impressions and product page views
  4. More views → more downloads (if conversion holds)
  5. More downloads → even stronger ranking signal

The reverse is also true. A low conversion rate tells Apple your page doesn't match search intent, and your rankings will gradually erode even if your keyword metadata is perfect. This is why improving your App Store ranking isn't just about keywords — it's about the entire page experience.

7 Ways to Improve Your App Store Conversion Rate

1. Make your first 3 screenshots do the selling

Users see your first three screenshots before scrolling. These should show your core value proposition in under 5 seconds. Use large text overlays (minimum 24pt equivalent) with benefit-driven copy, not feature lists. "Track 50+ currencies offline" beats "Currency converter with offline mode."

2. Front-load your description

Only the first 3 lines of your description are visible before the "more" tap. Put your strongest hook there. Lead with the problem you solve, not your company history. Apple indexes the full description for search, but users judge you on those first 170 characters.

3. Get your rating above 4.0 stars with 100+ reviews

Apps below 4.0 stars see conversion rates 20–30% lower than comparable apps above 4.5, per SplitMetrics 2025 benchmark data. Use the native SKStoreReviewController prompt at a moment of delight — after a completed task, not on first launch. Aim for 100+ ratings so the star display rounds favorably.

4. Add an App Preview video (15–30 seconds)

Product pages with a preview video convert 15–25% better than those without, according to Apple's developer guidance. Show real UI, not motion graphics. The first 5 seconds must demonstrate the core action. Autoplay in search results means your video also boosts impression-to-page-view rate.

5. Use Custom Product Pages for paid traffic

If you're running Apple Search Ads or sharing links on social media, create Custom Product Pages (CPPs) that match the ad's messaging. A user who clicks an ad about "offline mode" should land on a page highlighting offline mode — not your generic screenshots. CPPs typically lift conversion 10–20% versus the default page for matched traffic.

6. Localize screenshots and description for top markets

If 30% of your page views come from Japan but your screenshots are English-only, you're bleeding conversions. Localizing screenshots and the first 3 description lines for your top 3–5 territories can lift conversion 15–40% in those markets, based on Apptweak localization case studies.

7. Run A/B tests with Product Page Optimization

Apple's native Product Page Optimization (PPO) lets you test up to 3 icon variants, 3 screenshot sets, and 3 preview videos simultaneously. Test one element at a time. Run each test for at least 2 weeks or 1,000 conversions, whichever comes later. A single icon test moved conversion by 8% for a utility app we tracked in 2025.

How to Track Conversion Rate Over Time

Checking conversion rate once tells you where you are. Tracking it weekly tells you whether your changes are working. Here's a practical monitoring setup:

  1. Weekly check: App Store Connect → Analytics → Metrics → Conversion Rate. Compare week-over-week, not day-over-day (daily noise is high).
  2. Segment by source: Filter by App Store Search vs. Browse vs. Referrer. Search traffic converts highest (intent-driven). If your overall rate drops, check whether a low-intent source is diluting it.
  3. Correlate with ranking changes: If you're tracking keyword positions with a tool like AppClimb's rank tracker, overlay conversion rate data. A ranking jump without a conversion bump means you're ranking for the wrong keywords.
  4. Set a baseline: Record your conversion rate before any product page change. Wait 7 days after the change before comparing. Apple's data has a 48-hour lag.

Conversion Rate vs. Other App Store Metrics

Conversion rate doesn't live in isolation. Here's how it relates to the other metrics you'll see in App Store Connect:

Metric What It Measures Relationship to Conversion Rate
Impressions Times your app appears in search/browse results Top of funnel. More impressions → more page views → more conversion opportunities.
Product Page Views Times users open your product page Denominator of conversion rate. Impression-to-view rate measures listing appeal in search results.
Conversion Rate % of page visitors who download The metric this article covers. Measures page persuasiveness.
Retention Rate % of users still active after N days Post-install quality signal. Low retention can indirectly hurt rankings over time.
Crash Rate % of sessions with a crash High crash rates lead to negative reviews, which lower conversion rate.

The full funnel looks like this: Impressions → Page Views → Downloads → Active Users. Conversion rate sits at the critical hinge between visibility and growth. Common Conversion Rate Mistakes

  • Optimizing for the wrong metric. Chasing impressions while ignoring conversion is like pouring water into a leaky bucket. Fix the page first, then scale visibility.
  • Comparing across categories. A 25% conversion rate for a hardcore RPG is solid. The same rate for a calculator app is a red flag. Always benchmark within your category.
  • Ignoring the "already installed" effect. After a feature or press mention, your page views spike with existing users checking for updates. Your conversion rate will temporarily drop. Don't panic-optimize. Wait a week.
  • Testing too many things at once. If you change screenshots, description, and icon in the same update, you'll never know what moved the needle. Change one element, wait 7–14 days, then change the next.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good App Store conversion rate?

A good App Store conversion rate is 25–35% for most categories. Games average 25–30%, utilities 30–40%, and finance apps 20–25%. Anything above 35% is excellent. If your rate is below 20%, your product page likely has issues with screenshots, description, or rating social proof.

Where do I find my conversion rate in App Store Connect?

Open App Store Connect, go to Analytics, then select the Metrics tab. Choose "Conversion Rate" from the metrics dropdown. You can filter by source type, territory, and device. The conversion rate shown is calculated as Total Downloads divided by Total Product Page Views for your selected date range.

Does conversion rate affect App Store ranking?

Yes. Apple's ranking algorithm considers conversion rate as a quality signal. Apps that convert more visitors into downloads get ranked higher for the same keywords because Apple interprets high conversion as relevance. A 10-point conversion rate improvement can move you up 5–15 positions for competitive keywords.

What is the difference between conversion rate and install rate?

Conversion rate measures product page visitors who tap the download button. Install rate measures users who actually complete the installation after tapping download. Conversion rate is always higher than install rate because some users cancel mid-download or run out of storage. App Store Connect reports conversion rate; install rate requires third-party attribution tools.
